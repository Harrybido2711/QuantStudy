## Definition

**Alpha** ($\alpha$) is the component of a portfolio or security's return that cannot be explained by exposure to systematic risk factors. It represents the **value added by active management** beyond passive factor exposure.

$$
\alpha = r_p - \left(r_f + \beta(r_m - r_f)\right)
$$

In a multi-factor context:

$$
\alpha_i = r_i - \sum_{k} \hat{\beta}_{ik} \hat{f}_k
$$

## Types of Alpha

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Jensen's Alpha** | CAPM-based excess return |
| **Factor Alpha** | Return unexplained by all known factors |
| **Portable Alpha** | Alpha extracted from one strategy, applied to another |
| **Statistical Alpha** | Predictive edge from quantitative signals |

## Why It Matters

Alpha is the central question in active investing:

- **Zero-sum game**: Before costs, aggregate alpha across all investors is zero
- **After costs**: Most active managers destroy alpha through fees and transaction costs
- **True alpha decays**: As a signal is discovered, arbitrage erodes its edge

## Common Misconceptions

- **High returns ≠ alpha**: A strategy that returns 20% in a bull market might have zero alpha after accounting for market beta
- **Factor loading ≠ alpha**: Value investing captures the HML factor premium — this is risk compensation, not skill
- **Short-term alpha ≠ sustainable alpha**: Overfitting to historical data produces spurious alpha

## Relationship to Risk

The **Information Ratio** measures alpha quality:

$$
IR = \frac{\alpha}{\text{TE}} = IC \cdot \sqrt{BR}
$$

Where TE is tracking error, IC is the Information Coefficient, and BR is the number of independent bets (breadth).
